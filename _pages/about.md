I am a PhD Candidate in the Environmental Systems Graduate Group at the University of California, Merced (UC Merced). I am a member of the Earth Observation and Remote Sensing (EORS) Lab at UC Merced, and I am advised by Dr. Erin Hestir.

In my current research, I utilize active and passive remote sensing data from satellites, drones, and aircraft, as well as in-situ surveys of plants and macroinvertebrates, and collections of environmental DNA from soils, sediments, and water to assess, monitor, and model biodiversity. Given the intrinsic link between life and water, much of my research focuses on the unique ecosystems where biodiversity and water converge, such as floodplains, riparian forests, and ephemeral wetlands. I am also interested in how the human dimension (agriculture and urban development) converges with water and biodiversity, creating emerging challenges for management decisions that affect both people and ecosystems.

I am an advocate for the use of open-source geospatial code, tools, and data. I am most comfortable coding in R or Python, although I occasionally use Javascript and Bash for specific geospatial applications.

Background
======

My path to the PhD has been a non-standard one over the last decade, taking me through teen parenthood, service in the U.S. Army as a medic, and a period of balancing obtaining a community college degree in Physics at Modesto Junior College and work responsibilities before entry into a 4-year environmental engineering program at University of California, Merced (UC Merced).

As an undergraduate at UC Merced, I was able to gain research experiences in 3D printing and fabrication with the Rapid Prototyping Lab at UC Merced and air quality mitigation through a USDA-NRCS Pathways internship. However, it was during an undergraduate research position with the Earth Observation and Remote Sensing Lab at UC Merced under the mentorship of Dr. Erin Hestir that I developed a keen interest in my current research areas - remote sensing, geospatial data science, and spatial ecology. 

The realization that pursuit of remote sensing as a scientific discipline necessitated a graduate education led me to apply for the NSF Graduate Research Fellowship Program in 2021. The award of the NSF-GRFP changed my life and has led me to pursue doctoral studies in remote sensing with the lab that has cultivated my research interests throughout my academic career.
